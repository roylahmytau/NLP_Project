The Treaty of Abernethy (1072) was signed between William the Conqueror and King Malcolm III of Scotland. William invaded Scotland in 1072, riding as far as Abernethy where he met up with his fleet of ships. Malcolm submitted, paid homage to William and surrendered his son Duncan as a hostage, beginning a series of arguments as to whether the Scottish Crown owed allegiance to the King of England.

The Treaty of Saint Clair-sur-Epte was signed on 24 November 911, between King Charles III of West Francia and Rollo, a Viking leader. The treaty offered Rollo and his men the French lands between the river Epte and the Atlantic coast in exchange for their protection against further Viking incursions. The area corresponded to the northern part of present-day Upper Normandy down to the river Seine, but the Duchy would eventually extend west beyond the Seine.

The Norman invasion of England in 1066 is one of the most famous events in English history. It marked the transition from the Anglo-Saxon period to the Norman period of English history. The invasion began on 28 September 1066 with the Battle of Stamford Bridge and ended on 25 December 1066 with the death of Harold Godwinson and the crowning of William, Duke of Normandy, as King William I of England. The battle of Hastings, which was fought on 14 October 1066, was the most decisive and is often considered the turning point of the invasion.

William I of England, also known as William the Conqueror, was the first Norman king of England. He reigned from 1066 until his death in 1087. William was born in Falaise, Normandy, and became Duke of Normandy in 1035. He invaded England in 1066 and defeated the Anglo-Saxon king Harold Godwinson at the Battle of Hastings. William was crowned king on Christmas Day 1066. His reign saw the introduction of the feudal system and the construction of many castles across England. He died in 1087 and was buried in Caen, Normandy.
