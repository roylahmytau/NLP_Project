Q1: When did the church reform begin according to Document 12?
A1: In the early 11th century.
Q2: In which regions did Norman art primarily survive, and what were some of its characteristics?
A2: Norman art primarily survived in Britain as stonework or metalwork and in southern Italy as plentifully surviving forms strongly influenced by its Greek, Lombard, and Arab forebears, including mosaics and sculptured fonts.
Q3: How did the Normans' military activities contribute to their cultural impact in Europe and beyond?
A3: Norman military activities contributed to their cultural impact by founding the Kingdom of Sicily, leading to the Norman conquest of England, spreading their culture and military influence to the Crusader states in the Near East, and establishing presence in Scotland, Wales, Ireland, and the coasts of north Africa and the Canary Islands.
Q4: What were the key features of Norman architecture in England and southern Italy?
A4: In England, Norman architecture immediately succeeded Anglo-Saxon architecture and preceded Early Gothic. In southern Italy, the Normans incorporated elements of Islamic, Lombard, and Byzantine building techniques into their own, initiating a unique style known as Norman-Arab architecture.
Q5: Who were some of the prominent centers of church reform and art during the early 11th century in Normandy?
A5: Prominent centers of church reform and art during the early 11th century in Normandy included Mont-Saint-Michel, Fécamp, Jumièges, Bec, Saint-Ouen, Saint-Evroul, and Saint-Wandrille. These centers were in contact with the Winchester school, which channeled a pure Carolingian artistic tradition to Normandy.
